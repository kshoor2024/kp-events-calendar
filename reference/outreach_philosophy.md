# King Palm — Events Outreach Philosophy (v2.2)

Reference doc for Claude Cowork. This explains why we approach events the way we do, so Cowork's judgment stays aligned with how Brandon actually thinks about the business.

**This system is evergreen.** It runs every year, every week, forever. Today's date is always the anchor. We look forward only — never backward. When 2026 events become 2027 events become 2028 events, nothing about this logic changes.

---

## Core thesis

King Palm gets its best marketing ROI when real cannabis consumers use our product at real events where other consumers see them doing it.

Our outreach strategy:

**Lead by offering free product for their attendees and gift bags. Position it as value for their event, not a favor for us. Leave the door open for bigger conversations (booth, sponsorship, activation) so they can bring those up if they want — we evaluate case-by-case.**

We do not lead with "we can't pay for sponsorship" or "we're looking for free placement." That's how smaller brands sound. King Palm is in 5,000+ shops nationally — we reach out from a position of strength, offering product their attendees will love.

If they reply asking about sponsorship tiers or booth fees, Kate handles case-by-case with Brandon. Default is to entertain it, get the numbers, bring them back for review. Some events might be worth paying for that we'd never proactively pay for. We just never close that door in the first message.

## Two-track system

Every event that enters the database is one of two types:

**End User** — consumer-facing event. Gets full outreach treatment. Cowork drafts a message. Kate reviews and sends. We ship product if accepted. We track content received.

Examples: seshes, cups, cannabis-themed festivals, consumer music festivals (Rolling Loud, Coachella, Camp Flog Gnaw), dispensary parties, consumption lounge events, budtender appreciation events, 420/710 parties.

**Business** — trade show / industry / B2B event. Added to calendar for visibility only. Tagged "Do Not Outreach — Business". No draft is generated. Never surfaces in Kate's outreach queue.

Examples: CHAMPS, MJBizCon, TPE, NACS, Hall of Flowers, NECANN, Spannabis, ICBC Berlin, Mary Jane Berlin, InterTabac.

Both types stay in the database. The team has full visibility into what's happening in the industry. Kate just only works the End User side.

## What "a good End User event" looks like

An event is a strong fit when most of these are true:

1. **Attendees are cannabis consumers or cannabis industry insiders** (budtenders, growers, lounge operators)
2. **The format supports product placement** — gift bags, welcome kits, judge kits for cups, vendor-area sampling, artist/crew hospitality, sesh circles where product gets passed around
3. **On-site consumption is allowed (or strongly implied)** — product is most valuable when it gets used, not just shown
4. **There's a path to content back to us** — organizers who tag brands, photographers who shoot vendor areas, event recaps with brand mentions
5. **Ideally recurring** — one relationship that gets us into 12 events a year beats 12 one-off relationships
6. **Competitor brands (RAW, Zig-Zag, Swisher, Blazy Susan, Puffco, etc.) attend or sponsor** — if their marketing teams vetted the fit, ours should too

---

## What a "not a fit" event looks like

- General music festivals with no cannabis dimension (no consumption area, no cannabis vendor partners, no artist crossover)
- International events outside of what the Germany team handles
- "Wellness" events, yoga retreats, general lifestyle events — even if some attendees might use cannabis, the fit isn't strong enough to justify shipping
- Private parties with no cannabis theme
- Charity events where the ask is cash, not product (unless Brandon has pre-approved)
- 21+ bar nights branded "smoke friendly" but with no actual cannabis component

---

## Big festivals — pursue, don't skip

We do pursue big consumer festivals (Rolling Loud, Lollapalooza, Coachella, Camp Flog Gnaw, Smokers Club Festival, etc.). We just don't pursue them via the paid-vendor path. We pursue them via:

- Artist / VIP hospitality kits (tour buses, green rooms, artist gifting suites)
- Gift bag inclusions for specific tiers
- Backstage placements
- Existing brand activation programs where we can piggyback

Template 7 in outreach_templates.md handles this — confident, positions us as a national brand, leaves the door open for bigger conversations if the event wants to quote us.

## Competitor brand tracking

If RAW, Zig-Zag, Swisher Sweets, Blazy Susan, Backwoods, Juicy Jay's, Puffco, or similar brands are attending or sponsoring an event, that's a strong signal King Palm should be there too. Their marketing teams have already done the fit analysis for us — we follow their footprint.

Cowork watches their Instagram accounts weekly for event tags, venue mentions, story highlights, and sponsored event announcements. Events where they're active get added as End User with the "competitor-active" tag and priority A.

This is the single highest-quality sourcing signal we have. A new event that shows up on a competitor's feed is worth 10 events found through generic search.

## Priority ranking for End User events

**Tier A (work first):**
- Consumption lounges
- Dispensary-hosted events (especially recurring)
- Budtender appreciation events
- Recurring seshes
- Cannabis cups
- 420 / 710 parties at consumption-friendly venues
- Events where competitor brands (RAW, Zig-Zag, Blazy Susan, etc.) are active

**Tier B (work after A is clear):**
- Cannabis-branded music festivals (reggae, hip-hop, stoner rock)
- One-off 420 / 710 parties
- Brand activation events we're invited to
- Small concerts at cannabis-friendly venues

**Tier C (opportunistic only):**
- Bigger festivals (Cali Roots, Reggae on the River, Rolling Loud, Coachella) — via artist hospitality path, not paid vendor
- Crossover events (streetwear × cannabis, art × cannabis) — vet carefully
- Charity events (only with Brandon pre-approval)

**Never:**
- Anything on skip_list.md
- Any event tagged Business

## Volume targets (rough)

At a healthy cadence, the national pipeline should look like:

- 15-35 new events added per week to the database
- 8-20 of those are End User (get draft outreach)
- 30-60 active outreach threads at any given time
- 10-25 accepted per month (product shipped)
- 60-70% post-event content recovery (IG tags, photos, shoutouts)

If we're consistently below these, source more aggressively or add outreach hours. If we're consistently above these, we're probably accepting too many low-fit events — tighten filters.

## Voice for outreach drafts Cowork generates

Drafts should sound like Kate — warm, direct, confident. NOT like a corporate AI or like a small brand begging for a spot.

**Do:**
- Lead with what we're bringing (product their attendees will love)
- Use conversational contractions ("we'd love to", "I'm")
- Reference something specific about the event when possible
- Mention King Palm's scale (5,000+ shops nationally) as context, not a brag
- Leave the door open for bigger conversations ("also happy to talk about anything else that would make sense on your end")
- Close with a low-pressure next step

**Don't:**
- Use "free" as the opening — the fact that product is free is obvious in context
- Say "at no cost", "we can't pay", "we don't have budget" — never show our constraints
- Apologize or soften the ask ("I hope this isn't too much to ask")
- Long paragraphs about King Palm's history
- Phrases a human wouldn't say in a casual work message

The goal is that Kate reviews the draft and changes maybe 1-2 words before sending. Not rewrite the whole thing.

---

## Voice for digests Cowork writes

- **Direct.** "This is a strong fit because..." not "This may potentially be worth considering..."
- **Honest about uncertainty.** If contact info is unknown, write "Unknown — Kate to manually look up." Don't guess.
- **Prioritized.** Lead with what Kate should do Monday morning.
- **Unfluffy.** No marketing language ("premier," "must-see," "groundbreaking"). Plain description of what the event actually is.
- **Useful to Brandon specifically.** Assume the reader already knows the cannabis industry. Don't over-explain basics.
- **No padding.** If the week has 3 real opportunities, say "3 real opportunities." Don't inflate to 10 by including weak fits.

## What to do if the reply comes back asking for money / sponsorship

This is Kate's call with Brandon, not Cowork's. Default posture in the reply:

> "Appreciate you sending that over — let me review the package on our end and come back to you. In the meantime, can we still get product into {GIFT BAGS / HOSPITALITY / BUDTENDER KITS} for the event regardless of whether the bigger piece comes together?"

This keeps the free-product path alive while we evaluate. Some events might legitimately be worth paying for. We just never commit in round one of a conversation.

---

## One more note on tone — for Cowork specifically

When drafting outreach, don't write like the message is going to someone below our weight class. We're reaching out as King Palm — a serious national brand. Our tone is the tone of a brand that has options and is choosing to partner with this specific event.

If the draft would make Brandon cringe, it's wrong. If it sounds like something a confident operator at a real company would send, it's right.
